You Have to Carry Insurance to Drive. Why Don't They?

Every person who has ever registered a vehicle in Texas knows the deal. You want to put that truck on a public road — roads built with public money, maintained by public labor, used by a public that did not ask to share space with your two tons of steel — you pay State Farm. Not because you're a bad driver. Because risk, once released into a shared system, becomes everyone's problem. The cost of that risk belongs to whoever introduced it.

This is not a controversial idea. We settled it decades ago. It is so settled that most people don't think about it anymore. They just pay the premium and drive.

Now consider what happens when a company trains a large language model on the collected writing, art, code, conversation, and knowledge of billions of people — most of whom were never asked, never compensated, never consulted — and then releases that model onto the same shared infrastructure those people depend on: the internet, the power grid, the attention economy, the cognitive commons.

Where is the insurance?

There is a concept in ecology called the keystone species — an organism whose disproportionate effect on its environment holds the entire system together. Remove it, and the ecosystem doesn't just lose one species. It collapses in ways that are non-linear, cascading, and often irreversible. The concept came out of work done in tide pools in the Pacific Northwest in the 1960s, though indigenous communities along that same coast had understood the relational logic of ecosystems for generations before anyone wrote it into a journal.

The reason the keystone concept matters here is not biological. It's structural. Some things, when introduced into a complex system, change the rules for everyone else. You don't have to intend to change the rules. Your presence is enough.

Large language models are keystone interventions. They did not merely add a new tool to the internet. They changed what the internet is. They changed what text means, what trust costs, what verification requires. They changed the threat model for every person, organization, and institution that operates in digital space — which, in 2026, is everyone.

The companies that built and released these systems did not absorb those costs. They externalized them. Onto you.

Here is what individual defense now looks like, conservatively estimated:

A personal VPN and identity monitoring service. A password manager with breach alerts. An AI-detection layer for email phishing, which has become nearly undetectable since models learned to write in your colleague's voice from three leaked Slack messages. A deepfake detection subscription — because your face and your voice are now raw material without your consent. Regular security audits of the devices you depend on. Time — yours, uncompensated — spent developing the judgment to distinguish synthetic from real, safe from compromised, trustworthy from convincing.

Run the numbers honestly. Five to seven thousand dollars a year is not an aggressive estimate for a household that takes this seriously. That's before accounting for the productivity loss of operating under ambient suspicion of everything you read, every link you click, every voice message you receive from someone who sounds exactly like your mother.

None of this existed at this scale before mass model deployment. The risk was introduced by entities with trillion-dollar valuations. The cost is being paid by individuals with median incomes.

This is not bad luck. It is a structural transfer of liability.

There is a word for when a private entity profits by pushing costs onto a public that didn't consent to bear them. Economists call it externalization. The rest of us call it getting away with something.

The fossil fuel industry externalized the cost of a destabilized atmosphere. The opioid industry externalized the cost of addiction and death onto healthcare systems, families, and communities — particularly communities that had already been systematically denied resources. The tobacco industry externalized the cost of cancer. In each case, the argument against making them pay was the same: innovation, growth, jobs, the future. In each case, the people bearing the cost were not the people collecting the profit.

We are watching the same pattern run again, faster, with more compute.

The conversation about what to do about AI has mostly happened between governments, academics, and the companies themselves. Three groups that share a common incentive to deal in abstractions. Regulation frameworks. Safety benchmarks. Responsible deployment guidelines. Document after document produced by people whose personal exposure to AI-enabled harm is, at most, theoretical.

The people with non-theoretical exposure are everyone else.

This article is not a call for the government to protect you. Centralized protection against a decentralized, personalized, adaptive threat is a category error. The same researchers who built these systems will tell you that no single solution protects against the variety and volume of what's coming. What they are less eager to say out loud is that this means the cost of defense has to be distributed the same way the threat is — individually, locally, continuously.

That costs money. Someone has to provide it.

The insurance model is not a metaphor. It is a direct mechanism.

If you want to release a model capable of generating novel phishing attacks, synthetic identity fraud, automated manipulation at scale, and personalized social engineering — all demonstrated capabilities, not hypothetical ones — onto infrastructure shared by the public, you fund the public's defense. Not through a government intermediary that will spend a decade designing a framework before writing a check. Directly. To the people.

A levy on model deployment, proportional to scale of release and demonstrated risk surface, distributed as a security dividend to every person whose data was used in training and every person whose digital environment the model now operates in. Use the money however you need to: buy the monitoring services, pay someone to teach you how to recognize synthetic media, compensate yourself for the hours you spend on verification tasks that did not exist before.

This is not a radical idea. It is State Farm. It is the logic of liability that every Texan already lives with. You put your risk on my road, you carry coverage.

The truck analogy holds in one more important way. No one claims that requiring insurance prevents all accidents. It doesn't. What it does is ensure that the cost of the accident doesn't fall entirely on the person who was just trying to get where they were going.

That's all this is. Stop letting them drive uninsured.

The diversity of individual defenses is not a weakness in this model — it is the point. A monoculture defense, one solution deployed uniformly, is one exploitable pattern. A population of people with varied, self-selected, self-maintained defenses is far harder to attack systematically. The companies know this. They benefit from the robustness of the ecosystem they operate in. They should pay to maintain it.
